## 1. Context

Governance (`12`) authorizes operations; the Policy Engine (`20`) evaluates
rules; Execution Strategy (`13`) and Validation (`14`) carry approval and
validation policies. Across these, four problems block an implementable
governance model: an undefined policy language, an undefined conflict-resolution
metric, two different approval taxonomies, and a determinism claim that collides
with LLM and human steps.

## 2. Problem Statement

- **G12 â€” Policy mechanism undefined.** No condition/constraint language; the
  conflict-resolution metric "Specificity" is undefined; "Default Policy"
  referenced but never defined; Policy `Decision Types` (Retry/Escalate/Abort/
  Delay) overlap **Recovery** semantics.
- **G8 â€” Approval taxonomy mismatch.** Governance = {Automatic, Human Review,
  Explicit Approval}; Execution Strategy = {Automatic, Human Approval,
  Multi-stage, Deferred}. Governance evaluates the Strategy's approval policy, so
  they must share one vocabulary.
- **G13 â€” Dual ownership of recovery/validation strategy.** Skills (`06`) carry
  Recovery + Validation strategies; Execution Strategy (`13`) carries Recovery +
  Validation policies; Validation (`14`) owns completion. No precedence rule.
- **G14 â€” Determinism vs. cognition.** Intent Resolution (LLM), Execution (LLM
  runtimes), human approvals, and human/hybrid validation are non-deterministic,
  yet determinism is asserted platform-wide.

## 3. Decision

### 3.1 Policy is data-driven declarative rules (not an embedded DSL) (G12)

A **Policy** is a versioned object with a **structured condition tree** over
typed operational attributes (no embedded general-purpose/Turing-complete
language). Conditions are boolean combinations (`all`/`any`/`not`) of predicates
on named attributes (e.g. `risk_level`, `domain`, `workspace`, `cost_estimate`,
`actor`). Rationale: data-driven rules are deterministic, auditable, simulatable,
and need no sandboxed interpreter to secure.

- **Specificity** is **defined** as the number of bound attribute predicates a
  policy matches (a more specific policy constrains more attributes). Conflict
  resolution order (deterministic): **Specificity â†’ Priority â†’ Version â†’
  Default Policy**.
- The **Default Policy** is **defined**: a platform-level deny-by-default for
  governed actions when no policy matches (**fail closed**), and allow-by-default
  only for explicitly ungoverned action classes. (Honors the v1 "A-001 must fail
  closed" constraint.)

### 3.2 Policy Engine evaluates; Governance authorizes (boundary)

- **Policy Engine** = the deterministic evaluation *mechanism*. Given a request +
  applicable policies it returns a **Policy Decision** âˆˆ {`Allow`, `Deny`,
  `RequireApproval`, `Delay`, `Escalate`, `RequestInformation`}. It performs no
  action.
- **Governance** = the authority *domain*: owns governance policies, runs the
  **approval workflow**, and writes the **immutable audit** (which, per ADR-001,
  is governance decision **events** in the authoritative log).
- **Recovery strategies are not Policy Decisions (G12 narrowed).** The Policy
  Engine never returns `Retry`/`Rollback`/`Abort` as a *governance* outcome.
  Recovery (`19`) owns strategy selection; it *may consult* policy (e.g. "is a
  runtime switch permitted?") and receives Allow/Deny, but the recovery
  *strategy* is Recovery's, not the Policy Engine's. This removes the
  Decision-Type/Recovery overlap.

### 3.3 One approval taxonomy (G8)

A single platform vocabulary, used by **both** Governance and Execution Strategy:

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| `Automatic` | No human; policy authorizes (low risk). |
| `HumanReview` | One human approver required. |
| `MultiStage` | Multiple/sequential approvers or gates. |
| `Deferred` | Approval obtained at a later, defined checkpoint. |

Governance's "Explicit Approval" maps to `HumanReview` or `MultiStage` by risk.
Execution Strategy declares the required approval *level*; Governance evaluates
and enforces it. The two never use divergent terms.

### 3.4 Precedence for validation & recovery policy (G13)

When a Skill and an Execution Strategy both carry validation/recovery guidance:
**Execution Strategy is authoritative for the operation; Skill guidance is a
default that the Strategy may override.** Precedence (highest first):
**Governance/Policy (hard constraints) â†’ Execution Strategy â†’ Skill default.**
Validation *evaluation* (the verdict) is always owned by the **Validation layer**
regardless of who declared the policy; Recovery *strategy selection* is always
owned by **Recovery**. Declaration â‰  evaluation.

### 3.5 Determinism boundaries (G14)

The platform guarantees the determinism of **coordination and governance**, not
of **cognition**:

- **Deterministic (guaranteed):** policy evaluation, state transitions, conflict
  resolution, machine-checkable validation (tests/build/lint), event folding/
  replay (ADR-001).
- **Non-deterministic (explicitly bounded):** Intent Resolution (LLM), Execution
  on LLM runtimes (the docs' "attempt identical behavior," not a guarantee),
  human approval/review, LLM-as-judge validation.
- **Replay rule:** non-deterministic outputs are **captured as recorded event
  data** and never recomputed on replay (ADR-001 Â§3.6). Replaying an operation
  replays *decisions and recorded outputs*, reproducing the same governed result
  without re-running inference. Determinism is a property of the **operational
  spine**, not of the models inside it.

## 4. Alternatives Considered

- **Embedded scripting DSL for policies.** *Rejected:* non-deterministic risk,
  sandbox/security burden, hard to simulate and audit. Data-driven rules meet the
  determinism and auditability mandates.
- **Let the Policy Engine own recovery decisions** (honor `20`'s Decision Types).
  *Rejected:* conflates governance authorization with operational recovery; two
  owners for "what do we do on failure." Recovery owns strategy.
- **Two approval taxonomies with a mapping layer.** *Rejected:* a mapping layer
  is a permanent source of drift; one vocabulary is simpler and safer.
- **Claim platform-wide determinism.** *Rejected:* false given LLM/human steps;
  the capture-as-data boundary is the honest, implementable guarantee.

## 5. Trade-offs

- **Gain:** deterministic, auditable, simulatable governance; one approval
  vocabulary; clear Policy-vs-Governance-vs-Recovery boundaries; an honest,
  testable determinism contract.
- **Cost:** the structured condition language is less expressive than a DSL
  (some complex rules need new predicate types over time â€” a versioned, additive
  change); precedence rules must be taught and enforced.
- **Accepted because:** governance correctness and replayability outweigh policy
  expressiveness; expressiveness can grow additively.

## 6. Consequences

- **AP-205** builds a data-driven evaluation engine with the defined specificity/
  precedence/default-policy semantics and side-effect-free simulation.
- **AP-406** integrates Governance authorization at the orchestration gate; audit
  = governance events in the log (ADR-001).
- **AP-308** Execution Strategy emits approval level using the single taxonomy.
- **AP-502/503** Validation evaluates; Recovery selects strategy; neither is a
  Policy Decision.
- **Contracts:** `policy.md` freezes the condition-tree shape, decision set,
  conflict order, and default-policy/fail-closed rule.

## 7. Risks

- **Predicate gaps** (a needed rule can't be expressed). *Mitigation:* additive,
  versioned predicate vocabulary; never an ad-hoc code path.
- **Fail-closed friction** (legitimate actions blocked when no policy matches).
  *Mitigation:* explicit ungoverned-action classes; policy simulation before
  rollout (AP-205).
- **Approval stalls with no timeout** (operational, R-10). *Mitigation:*
  `Deferred`/escalation timeouts defined in Execution Strategy; Recovery handles
  approval-timeout as a failure category.

## 8. Validation Strategy

- **Determinism test:** identical request + policies â†’ identical decision, every
  run (AP-205).
- **Conflict-resolution truth table:** specificityâ†’priorityâ†’versionâ†’default
  produces the documented winner for crafted overlaps (AP-205).
- **Fail-closed test:** a governed action with no matching policy is denied and
  audited (AP-406).
- **Taxonomy conformance:** Governance and Execution Strategy emit/consume only
  the four approval terms (AP-308/406).
- **Replay-without-reinference test:** replaying an operation reproduces governed
  outcomes from captured data without invoking any model (ADR-001/AP-203).

## 9. Migration Considerations

- **Nexus v1** stamps governance flags server-side from a static `_ACTION_POLICY`
  table and enforces "A-001 fail closed." That table is the seed Policy set;
  migrate each entry to a structured Policy with explicit conditions and the
  fail-closed Default Policy.
- v1 approval gates (Discord approval channel) become `HumanReview`/`MultiStage`
  approval workflows emitting governance events.
- v1's deterministic, server-side flag stamping aligns with the determinism
  boundary â€” cognition (the chat LLM) never sets governance flags; only the
  Policy Engine does.

## 10. Future Evolution

- Adaptive/learned governance and policy packs (deferred) must remain
  **deterministic at evaluation time** â€” learning may *propose* policy versions,
  but evaluation stays data-driven and replayable.
- Delegated approvals, policy simulation dashboards, and risk-model-driven
  `risk_level` computation attach as new predicates/inputs without changing the
  evaluation contract.
- An attestation/trust model for capability claims (ADR-002 future) can feed new
  policy predicates.
